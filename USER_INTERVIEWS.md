## Interview 1

I spoke with my mother, who is not deeply technical and mostly represents a normal everyday software user. I wanted to understand whether the product actually made sense to someone outside engineering circles. When I showed her the idea, her first reaction was, “I still don’t understand why there are so many AI tools doing similar things.” She also said, “If this asks for too much information, most people will leave immediately.”

One thing that surprised me was how strongly she reacted to trust signals. Before even discussing features, she asked whether the site would sell user information or secretly charge later. That changed how I approached the landing page. I added clearer messaging like “No login required” and made the audit process feel simpler and more transparent.

Another useful thing she pointed out was that normal users do not think in terms of “optimization” or “AI infrastructure.” They think in terms of confusion and wasted subscriptions. That pushed me to simplify a lot of wording throughout the product and make the recommendations much shorter and easier to understand.

## Interview 2

I spoke with my father, who is generally very skeptical of online tools and subscriptions. I intentionally showed him the project because I wanted honest criticism instead of validation. His first response was, “Most tools like this are just trying to collect leads.” He also said, “If the savings numbers look unrealistic, nobody serious will trust it.”

The most surprising thing he said was, “Sometimes telling someone they are already spending correctly builds more trust than forcing savings everywhere.” That completely changed part of my audit engine logic. Earlier, I was tempted to make every audit show dramatic optimization opportunities, but after this conversation I added logic where some users are told their setup already looks reasonable.

He also pushed me to focus more on credibility than flashy design. He repeatedly asked where the pricing data came from and whether the recommendations were actually defendable. That led to the idea of creating PRICING_DATA.md with source links and keeping the audit calculations rule-based instead of AI-generated.

## Interview 3

I spoke with my sister, who is curious about AI tools and regularly experiments with ChatGPT and coding assistants casually. Her feedback was very different from my parents because she focused much more on user experience and interaction.

One thing she said was, “This should feel more like talking to an assistant instead of filling a boring form.” She also mentioned, “People will share this if the results page looks cool enough.” Another thing she pointed out was that younger users expect more visual feedback and smoother onboarding instead of large blocks of forms.

The most surprising part of the conversation was that she cared less about the actual savings amount and more about how interactive and personalized the product felt. That directly inspired the conversational onboarding idea where AI helps users fill out the audit instead of forcing them through a traditional form flow.

Her feedback also pushed me to improve the shareable results page, add stronger visuals, and think more about virality rather than only backend logic and calculations.
