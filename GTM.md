# Exact Target User

The exact target user is:

A technical founder or engineering manager at a seed to Series A startup with 5–40 employees that actively uses multiple AI tools across engineering and operations.

More specifically:

* CTOs at AI-native startups,
* engineering managers managing tool budgets,
* founders personally paying SaaS bills,
* and infra-conscious startups scaling fast after initial product-market fit.

These teams usually adopted AI tooling quickly without central purchasing decisions. Over time they accumulate overlapping subscriptions across ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, Copilot, Gemini, and direct API spend without realizing how fragmented or inefficient it became.

This product is less useful for enterprises with procurement teams and less useful for solo hobby users spending $20/month.

## First 100 Users With $0 Paid Budget

I would not rely on generic “build in public” posting.

Instead:

1. Create believable benchmark screenshots from the tool.
2. Post short teardown-style posts:

   * “A 12-person startup wasting $7k/year on AI tooling”
   * “Most teams are accidentally double-paying for AI.”
3. Share these in relevant Reddit threads where people already discuss tooling costs.
4. DM founders or engineering managers already complaining about AI spend publicly on X.
5. Submit to Hacker News with a practical angle, not marketing language.
6. Offer free manual audits to a few startup founders in exchange for feedback.
7. Ask those users to share their anonymized savings screenshots publicly.

The key is making the tool feel useful before trying to “market” it.

## The Unfair Distribution Channel

Credex’s unfair advantage is access to discounted AI infrastructure credits and real pricing visibility.

Most competitors can tell users:
“you might save money.”

Credex can say:
“we can directly help you capture those savings.”

That makes the audit tool much stronger because it is connected to an actual fulfillment layer instead of just analytics.

Another advantage is access to real-world AI purchasing behavior across startups. Over time that could create proprietary benchmark data competitors would not easily have.

## What Week-1 Traction Looks Like

Realistically, strong week-1 traction would look like:

* 300–600 landing page visitors,
* 80–120 completed audits,
* 20–35 captured leads,
* 5–10 consultation requests,
* 1–3 companies seriously discussing credits.

For a niche B2B infrastructure-adjacent tool, that would already be a strong signal.

The important metric is not raw traffic. It is whether founders actually trust the output enough to share contact information or discuss cost optimization further.
